\section*{Introduction}
In this laboratory experiment, the effect of transmission lines on high-frequency signals
will be investigated. One focus is to gain an understanding of termination and reflections.

% \end{titlepage}



\section{Theory}
A transmission line can be viewed as a series of many infinitesimal LRC circuits.
These combine to a total resistance $R$, inductance $L$, capacitance $C$ and transconductance $G$.
The transmission line's characteristic impedance, i.\,e. the frequency-dependent ratio of voltage to current,
is then \cite{manual-theory}
\begin{equation*}
  Z_0(\omega) = \sqrt\frac{j\omega L+R}{j\omega C+G}.
\end{equation*}
In the lossless case, $R$ and $G$ are $0$ and the characteristic impedance is independent of frequency.
This is assumed to be approximately true here.

If there is a discontinuity in the impedance at the end of a transmission line, reflections occur \cite{manual-theory}.
The reflection coefficient is the ratio of the refelcted voltage $V_r$ to the incoming voltage $V_0$
and can be calculated as \cite{manual-theory}
\begin{equation}
  r = \frac{V_r}{V_0} = \frac{Z-Z_0}{Z+Z_0} \label{eq:reflection-coefficient}
\end{equation}
where $Z$ is the impedance at the end of the transmission line, also called the termination impedance.
Putting an impedance at the end of a transmission line is called
parallel termination, because the impedance is connected in parallel to the capacitors and transconductances
of the transmission line.



\section{The Experiment}
We produce a high-frequency square wave using a signal generator and send this signal through transmission lines
with various kinds of terminations, then view the result on an oscilloscope.

The signal generator is set to TTL mode which enables reliable generation of higher frequencies.
In this case a frequency of \SI{600}{\kHz} is used.


\subsection{Effect of Different Terminations}\label{Setup}
First we connect the function generator to the oscilloscope using a coaxial BNC cable with a bifurcation (T-piece)
at the end. The oscilloscope is connected to one output of the T-piece and the other output is left open.
Looking at the start of a square pulse, we see the voltage level quickly rise to about \SI{2}{\V},
see \figref{fig:short-coax-t-open}.

% The oscilloscope is connected to one output of the T-piece and a second coaxial cable is connected to
% the other output. When leaving the end of the cable open, the start of a square pulse looks like \figref{fig:}

Now we connect a second coaxial cable to the previously open output of the T-piece and terminate the cable
with a \SI{50}{\ohm} resistor.
The square wave now reaches only about half the amplitude of before, see \figref{fig:short-coax-t-coax-50}.
This can be explained as follows: With the open T-piece, the termination resistance is infinite and the
reflection coefficient is $1$ according to \eqref{eq:reflection-coefficient}. Since the two outputs of the
T-piece are close together, the reflection reaches the oscilloscope almost at the same time as the original
signal and the two voltages add to twice the amplitude of the original signal. On the other hand,
when we connect the second coaxial cable and leave terminate it with its characteristic impedance ($Z=Z_0=\SI{50}{\ohm}$)
the signal splits at the T-piece and the part that travels down the coaxial cable is not reflected (because $r=0$).
On the oscilloscope we only see the incoming signal so the amplitude is half of what we saw with the reflection.

Next, we leave the end of the second coaxial cable open. The resulting waveform can be seen in
\figref{fig:short-coax-t-coax-open}. We see that first the signal rises to a stable level and 
about \SI{12}{\ns} later it rises again to a higher level. This is because first we measure
only the original signal that travels down the end of the T-piece connected to the oscilloscope.
After the original signal travels down the second transmission line,
it is reflected positively at the end ($r=1$) and travels back until it reaches the T-piece again,
adding to the measured signal. Because the coaxial cable has a delay of \SI{6}{\ns}, this takes
$2\cdot\SI{6}{\ns} = \SI{12}{\ns}$. In the picture, we additionally see effects of the oscilloscope's
termination when measuring the signal: It is set to \SI{1}{\Mohm} which deforms the signal
and enlarges it, the latter happening because the resistance of the oscilloscope essentially acts
as a voltage divider, where \SI{1}{\Mohm} makes up almost the full total resistance whereas
the usual termination of \SI{50}{\ohm} is only half of the total resistance.
In all other pictures, the oscilloscope's
termination is set to \SI{50}{\ohm} to match the characteristic impedance of the transmission line.
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}{.49\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{short-coax-t-coax-open}
    \caption{Start of a square pulse with an open coaxial cable connected to a T-piece.}
    \label{fig:short-coax-t-coax-open}
  \end{minipage}\hfill%
  \begin{minipage}{.49\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{short-coax-t-coax-short}
    \caption{Start of a square pulse with a shorted coaxial cable connected to a T-piece.}
    \label{fig:short-coax-t-coax-short}
  \end{minipage}
\end{figure}

When shorting the end of the second coaxial cable instead of leaving it open, the signal looks like
\figref{fig:short-coax-t-coax-short}. The effect is the same as before but the resistance is now
$Z \sim \SI{0}{\ohm}$ so the reflection coefficient is $\sim -1$,
so after \SI{12}{\ns} the voltage level drops down instead of rising.
It does not fully drop to $0$, suggesting that the piece used to short the end actually has
a non-negligible resistance, perhaps due to a bad connection of the shorting wire.



\subsection{Determining Characteristic Impedances}
In the second part of the experiment, we investigate two transmission lines of
unknown characteristic impedance. The goal is to find out their characteristic impedances by
changing the termination resistance until there is no reflection. In order to do this,
we connect the end of the respective cable to a variable resistor by means of a breadboard.
The oscilloscope is also connected to the breadboard,
replicating the setup with the T-piece that we had previously.

First, we use a long coaxial cable. 
With the variable resistor set to \SI{0}{\ohm} (i.e. a short circuit),
the signal looks like \figref{fig:long-coax-short}.
Again, the signal rises to a stable level before falling back down later on due to the negative reflection
reaching the oscilloscope. We can see the much longer delay of about \SI{500}{\ns}.
The delay is so long that the reflection is still present for a significant amount of time after the original
signal goes away, making the voltage drop below $0$.
The shape of the reflection is much softer than for the corter coaxial cable used previously.
This is possibly because losses are not negligible anymore due to the length of the cable,
so the characteristic impedance is now frequency dependent. Another effect may be the breadboard which
uses wires that are not shielded like the coaxial cable.
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}{.49\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{long-coax-short}
    \caption{Square pulse with a coaxial cable terminated with a variable resistor at \SI{0}{\ohm}.}
    \label{fig:long-coax-short}
  \end{minipage}\hfill%
  \begin{minipage}{.49\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{long-coax-termination}
    \caption{Square pulse with a coaxial cable terminated with a variable resistor at $\sim \SI{45}{\ohm}$.}
    \label{fig:long-coax-termination}
  \end{minipage}
\end{figure}

We vary the resistor until the reflection disappears, see \figref{fig:long-coax-termination}.
This happens at about \SI{45}{\ohm} meaning that the characteristic impedance of the cable
roughly has this value. Even with the termination resistance, we still see artifacts which
are again likely due to insufficient shielding of the wires on the breadboard.

Next, we use a twisted-pair cable (Ethernet cable). \figref{fig:long-twistedpair-open}
shows the signal when using a large termination resistance of \SI{20}{\kohm}, having a similar effect to
an open-ended cable. We can again see the positive reflection.

\figref{fig:long-twistedpair-termination} shows that the reflection disappears with a termination resistance
of \SI{100}{\ohm}, so this is the characteristic impedance of the cable.



\section{Conclusion}
This experiment examined the effect of transmission lines and their termination on high-frequency signals.
First, different termination resistances were used and we could see that high resistances lead to positive
reflections whereas low resistances lead to negative ones. This also showed the delaying effect of the
cable used. When terminating a transmission line with its own characteristic impedance,
there is no reflection. This fact was also used to find the characteristic impedance of two different
cables: The characteristic impedance of the coaxial cable was found to be $\sim \SI{45}{\ohm}$ whereas
the impedance of the twisted-pair cable was \SI{100}{\ohm}.
