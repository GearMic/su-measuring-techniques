\section*{Introduction}
Amplitude modulation (AM) is an important technique in communications systems, enabling
the use of a specific carrier frequency to transmit signals of different frequencies.

In this experiment, an amplitude modulation circuit will be used to modulate
a carrier voltage with a modulation signal and the resulting signal will be analyzed.
The signal will be demodulated using a diode rectifier.

% \end{titlepage}



\section{Theory}
Amplitude modulation involves a carrier voltage $V_c(t) = A\cos{\omega_c t}$, 
and a modulation signal $S(t) = m\cos{\omega_m t}$,
where the former must be of a higher frequency than the latter.
An amplitude-modulated signal is obtained by adding the carrier multiplied by the modulation
to the original carrier signal:
\begin{equation}
  V(t) = V_c(t)+ V_c(t)\cdot S(t)= A\sin{\omega_c t} (1+m\cos{\omega_m t}) \label{eq:am-formula}
\end{equation}
Where we must have $m\leq 1$ so that the sign of the voltage is never flipped by the modulation.
Using trigonometric identities, \eqref{eq:am-formula} can be brought to the form \cite{manual}
\begin{equation}
  V(t) = A \cos{\omega_c t} + \frac{m}{2}A
    \left[\cos((\omega_c-\omega_m)t) + \cos((\omega_c+\omega_m)t)\right]. \label{eq:am-simplified}
\end{equation}
From this we can see that the fourier transform of the modulated signal will
contain the original carrier frequency along with side bands shifted left and right
by the amount of the modulation frequency. These side bands can never have more than
half the amplitude of the carrier band because $m\leq 1$.



\section{Setup}\label{Setup}
The main part of the used amplitude modulation circuit is shown in \figref{fig:am-circuit}.
The central component is a CA3080 transconductance amplifier, which produces an output current
proportional to the voltage $v_i$ at its input and the Amplifier Bias Control current $i_{ABC}$.
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.65\textwidth]{am-circuit}
  \caption{Schematic of the main part of the amplitude modulation circuit \cite{manual}.}
  \label{fig:am-circuit}
\end{figure}
$v_i$ comes from the carrier voltage TE and $i_{ABC}$ is produced by
amplifying the modulation voltage TF using a 741 operational amplifier (op amp; 741-1)
and converting it to a current using a resistor. The output currect $i_o$ then depends
on the product of TE and TF and is converted to the desired modulated voltage
using another op amp (741-2).

The amplitude of the carrier and the degree of modulation can be adjusted using trimmer potentiometers 
\texttt{Amplitude adjust} and \texttt{Modulator trim} on the board. Due to issues with the circuit,
the carrier may also be reshaped when adjusting its amplitude. For this reason,
the carrier voltage was closer to a square shape than a sine wave
in our case.

We generate the modulation signal using a waveform generator and adjust the trimmers to get
a strong degree of modulation.
We view the modulated voltage along with the modulation signal on the oscilloscope, see \figref{fig:modulation350}.
\begin{figure}[htp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.6\textwidth]{modulation350}
  \caption{

    math 2 (purple): FFT of the modulated voltage with a cursor (\texttt{f1}) at the main frequency band.
  }
  \label{fig:modulation350}
\end{figure}
The modulated signal noticeably varies in amplitude at the same frequency and phase
as the modulation. The carrier frequency, which is significantly higher than the modulation
frequency, can be seen. The oscilloscope's fast fourier transform (FFT) function shows
the carrier frequency to be at around \SI{2.4}{\kHz}.

We see that the modulated signal always follows the modulation,
even when varying the modulation frequency, see \figref{fig:modulation-comparison}.

With a modulation frequency of \SI{350}{\Hz}, we consider the FFT 
of the modulated voltage, see \figref{fig:fft350}.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}{.5\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fft350}
  \end{minipage}%
  \begin{minipage}{.5\textwidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fft350-1}
  \end{minipage}
  \caption{
    FFT of the signal modulated at \SI{350}{\Hz} with cursors for the carrier band
    and the first side band on the left (left picture) and right (right picture).
    }
  \label{fig:fft350}
\end{figure}
We see a main peak at the carrier frequency, approximately \SI{2.4}{\kHz},
as well as multiple side bands. We measure the distance to the first side bands
to the left and right using cursors on the oscilloscope. The distance between the
cursors is displayed as \texttt{\Delta f} and is roughly \SI{350}{\Hz} corresponding
to the modulation frequency, as expected from \eqref{eq:am-simplified}.

The fact that there are more side bands is likely
due to the modulated wave being closer in shape to a square wave
than a sine, meaning that it contains more overtones. The asymmetry of the side
bands, with the left ones having a smaller amplitude than the right, is also due
to the imperfect shape of the wave.

We can see that the largest side band
has an amplitude of \SI{43.68}{\mV} while the carrier band has an amplitude of
\SI{343.4}{\mV} making for a ratio $\sim 0.13$. When adjusting the amplitude
of the modulation this ratio always stays significantly below $0.5$, as expected
from \eqref{eq:am-simplified}. The fact that it never even reaches close to $0.5$
can be explained by problems with the circuit, making the signal break
down when trying to achieve a higher degree of modulation.
For this reason we also cannot test what happens to the spectrum when trying to
increase the ratio above $0.5$.



\section{Demodulation Experiment}
To demodulate the signal, we use a diode feeding a resistor and capacitor
connected in parallel, as shown in \figref{fig:demodulation-circuit}.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \begin{minipage}{.62\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{demodulation-circuit}
    \caption{The diode circuit used for demodulation, in combination with a low-pass filter.}
    \label{fig:demodulation-circuit}
  \end{minipage}\hfill%
  \begin{minipage}{.36\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{demodulation-waveform}
    \caption{Example demodulated waveform.}
    \label{fig:demodulation-waveform}
  \end{minipage}
\end{figure}
When connecting the modulated voltage as $U_\text{in}$, the diode works as a rectifier,
cutting off the negative parts and leading to the individual peaks seen in \figref{fig:demodulation-waveform}.
We use a Germanium
diode which has a low threshold voltage \cite{manual} in order to remove as little of the positive
signal as possible. The capacitor $C_1$ is charged instantaneously by the voltage peaks but when the
voltage drops again, it discharges through $R_1$ in an exponential drop-off with time constant
$\tau = R_1C_1$. This leads to the demodulation circuit's output $U_1$ having the enveloping shape
seen in \figref{fig:demodulation-waveform}. This kind of signal is mostly made up of the modulation
frequency with only little carrier frequency content left, meaning that the signal has been demodulated.

The exponential drop-off should be slow enough that it does not closely follow the shape of the peaks,
but fast enough so that changes can still happen at the frequency of the modulation.
The time constant therefore needs to be on the order of the period of the carrier frequency,
$T = \frac{1}{\SI{2.4}{\kHz}} \sim \SI{400}{\micro\s}$.

First, we only use the demodulation circuit, i.e. the output is $U_1$ in \figref{fig:demodulation-circuit}.
The result with $\tau \approx \SI{2.5}{\ms}$ is shown in \figref{fig:demodulation-1}. 
\begin{figure}[htp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.65\textwidth]{demodulation-2.2uF-1.14kOhm.jpg}
  \caption{Demodulation with $C_1 = \SI{2.2}{\uF}$ and $R_1 = \SI{1.14}{\kohm}$.}
  \label{fig:demodulation-1}
\end{figure}
$\tau$ is significantly larger than $T$ and we see that some of the shape of the modulation signal is lost
in the long exponential decays. In this case, there were also problems with the modulation circuit
which is why the modulated signal is distorted in some places.

Demodulation with $\tau \approx \SI{390}{\us}$ is shown in \figref{fig:demoulation-2}.
\begin{figure}[htp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.65\textwidth]{demodulation-100nF-3.9kOhm-lowpass.jpg}
  \caption{Demodulation with $C_1 = \SI{100}{\nF}$ and $R_1 = \SI{3.9}{\kohm}$.}
  \label{fig:demoulation-2}
\end{figure}
This time, we also included the low-pass filter shown in \figref{fig:demodulation-circuit},
however this appears not to have made a significant difference. Likely the cutoff frequency
was too high.
The signal follows the individual peaks more closely than before.
Most of the carrier-frequency content is cut away,
though there are still significant variations over one period of the carrier signal.
Ideally, the time constant should thus be chosen slightly larger than \SI{390}{\ns},
which was not possible with the components available during the experiment.

\section{Conclusion}
In this experiment, an amplitude modulation circuit was used to modulate a carrier voltage with a 
modulation signal. The main frequency components of the resulting signal were inspected using
the FFT function of an oscilloscope. As expected, it was found that the main frequency component
is at the carrier frequency with side bands shifted up and down by the modulation frequency.

The modulated signal was demodulated using a circuit containing a diode, a capacitor and a resistor.
To improve the quality of the demodulated signal, different $RC$ time constants were tested and
and an additional low-pass filter was used.
