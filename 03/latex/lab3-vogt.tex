\section*{Introduction}
Amplitude modulation (AM) is an important technique in communications systems, enabling
the use of a specific carrier frequency to transmit signals of different frequencies.

In this experiment, an amplitude modulation circuit will be used to modulate
a carrier voltage with a modulation signal and the resulting signal will be analyzed.
The signal will be demodulated using a diode rectifier.

% \end{titlepage}



\section{Theory}
Amplitude modulation involves a carrier voltage $V_c(t) = A\cos{\omega_c t}$, 
and a modulation signal $S(t) = m\cos{\omega_m t}$,
where the former must be of a higher frequency than the latter.
An amplitude-modulated signal is obtained by adding the carrier multiplied by the modulation
to the original carrier signal:
\begin{equation}
  V(t) = V_c(t) (1+S(t)) = A\sin{\omega_c t} (1+m\cos{\omega_m t}) \label{eq:am-formula}
\end{equation}
We must have $m\leq 1$ so that the sign voltage is never flipped by the modulation.
Using trigonometric identities, \eqref{eq:am-formula} can be brought to the form \cite{manual}
\begin{equation}
  V(t) = A \cos{\omega_c t} + \frac{m}{2}A
    \left[\cos((\omega_c-\omega_m)t) + \cos((\omega_c+\omega_m)t)\right]. \label{eq:am-simplified}
\end{equation}
From this we can see that the fourier transform of the modulated signal will
contain the original carrier frequency along with side bands shifted left and right
by the amount of the modulation frequency. These side bands can never have more than
half the amplitude of the carrier band because $m\leq 1$.



\section{Setup}\label{Setup}
The main part of the amplitude modulation circuit is shown in \figref{fig:am-circuit}.
\begin{figure}

  \caption{Schematic of the main part of the amplitude modulation circuit \cite{manual}.}
  \label{fig:am-circuit}

The central component is a CA3080 transconductance amplifier, which produces an output current
proportional to the voltage $v_i$ at its input and the Amplifier Bias Control current $i_{ABC}$.
$v_i$ comes from the carrier voltage TE and $i_{ABC}$ is produced by
amplifying the modulation voltage TF using a 741 operational amplifier (op amp; 741-1)
and converting it to a current using a resistor. The output currect $i_o$ is then
proportional to the product of TE and TF\todo{actually $V_c(1+V_m)$?} and is converted to the desired modulated voltage
using another op amp (741-2).

the amplitude of the carrier and the degree of modulation can be adjusted using trimmer potentiometers 
\texttt{Amplitude adjust} and \texttt{Modulator trim} on the board. Due to issues with the circuit,
the carrier may also be reshaped when adjusting its amplitude. For this reason,
the carrier and modulated voltage was closer to a square shape than a sine wave
in our case.

We generate the modulation signal using a waveform generator and adjust the potentiometers to get
a strong degree of modulation.
We view the modulated voltage along with the modulation signal on the oscilloscope, see \figref{fig:modulation350}.
\begin{figure}[h]

The modulated signal noticeably varies in amplitude at the same frequency and phase
as the modulation. The carrier frequency, which is significantly higher than the modulation
frequency, can be seen. The oscilloscope's fast fourier transform (FFT) function shows
the carrier frequency to be at around \SI{2.4}{\kHz}.

We see that the modulated signal always follows the modulation frequency,
see \figref{fig:modulation-comparison}.

We see a main peak at the carrier frequency, approximately \SI{2.4}{\kHz}
As well as multiple side bands. We measure the distance to the first side bands
to the left and right using cursors on the oscilloscope. The distance between the
cursors is displayed as \texttt{\Delta f} and is roughly \SI{350}{\Hz} corresponding
to the modulation frequency, as expected from \eqref{eq:am-simplified}.

The fact that there are more side bands of significant amplitude is likely
due to the fact that the modulated wave is closer in shape to a square wave
than a sine, meaning that it contains more overtones. The asymmetry of the side
bands, with the left ones having a smaller amplitude than the right is also due
to the imperfect shape of the wave.

We can see that the largest side band
has an amplitude of \SI{43.68}{\mV} while the carrier band has an amplitude of
\SI{343.4}{\mV} making for a ratio $\sim 0.13$. When adjusting the amplitude
of the modulation this ratio always stays significantly below $0.5$, as expected
from \eqref{eq:am-simplified}. The fact that it never even reaches close to $0.5$
can be explained by problems with the circuit, making the signal break
down when trying to achieve a higher degree of modulation.
For this reason we also cannot test what happens to the spectrum when trying to
increase the ratio above $0.5$.
