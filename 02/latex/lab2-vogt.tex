\section*{Introduction}
Noise can occur in operational amplifiers (op amps; OPA) due to various effects of their internal function.
This noise is generally dependent on the frequency of operation.
The amount of noise present at a specific frequency is quantified by the \textit{spot noise density}
and the goal of our experiment is to measure this quantity for a Burr-Brown OPA37 op amp.

% \end{titlepage}



\section{Setup}\label{setup}
The op amp is set up in a non-inverting amplifier circuit, see \figref{fig:opa-circuit}.
The expected gain is $G=\frac{U_\text{in}}{U_\text{out}} = 1+\frac{R_f}{R_g}=1001$.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{opa-circuit}
  \caption{non-inverting amplifier circuit. $R_f = \SI{101}{\kohm}, R_g = \SI{101}{\ohm}$ \cite{manual}}
  \label{fig:opa-circuit}
\end{figure}

The circuit is contained on a printed circuit board (PCB) which has a metal casing
to shield against noise from electromagnetic fields. Additionally to the connections shown 
in the diagram, the op amp requires a positive and a negative supply voltage which
is provided here by \SI{9}{\V} batteries.

The capacitor in parallel to $R_f$ effectively lowers the resistance and therefore the gain for high frequencies,
acting as a low pass filter.
This prevents aliasing effects due to sampling of high frequencies \cite{manual}.
The input of the circuit is fed by a signal generator and the output is measured using an oscilloscope.



\section{Gain Measurement}
First, the gain of the circuit is measured by comparing the
input signal from the signal generator and the amplified output on the oscilloscope.
Two measurements are shown in \figref{fig:gain-measurement}.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}{.5\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{gain1.jpg}
  \end{minipage}%
  \begin{minipage}{.5\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{gain2.jpg}
  \end{minipage}
  \caption{Gain measurements of the operational amplifier circuit.
    The input to the circuit is on channel 1 (yellow), the output on channel 2 (blue)
    and \textit{Math1} (orange) shows the average of channel 1 over time.}
  \label{fig:gain-measurement}
\end{figure}

Due to the high gain of the op amp circuit, the input has a low amplitude and thus a
low signal-to-noise ratio.
To avoid exaggerating the measured peak-to-peak amplitude of the input due to noise,
the average of multiple waveforms is taken. The measured amplitudes still vary over time.
To calculate the measured gain, the mean values of the amplitudes of the two
measurements in \figref{fig:gain-measurement} are used:
\begin{equation*}
  G_\text{exp} = \frac{\overline{U}_\text{out,\,pp}}{\overline{U}_\text{in,\,pp}} = \frac{\SI{5.72}{\V} + \SI{5.64}{\V}}{\SI{5.404}{\mV} + \SI{5.368}{\mV}} \approx 1058
\end{equation*}
The experimental value is about \SI{6}{\percent} larger than the value of $1001$ calculated from the resistor values.
This is likely due to imprecision of the given values.



\section{Voltage Spot Noise Measurement}
The main task of the experiment is to measure the circuit's output voltage spot noise
$E_O$ at a frequency of \SI{1}{\kHz} and calculate the op amp's input noise voltage $E_\text{NI}$ from it.
To do this, the waveform generator is turned off and the op amp circuit's input connected to ground.
The output is again measured on the oscilloscope, which can be configured to calculate the fast fourier transform (FFT)
of the waveform. After some experimentation we settle on the transform parameters shown in \figref{fig:fourier-params}.
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{noise-opa3.jpg}
  \caption{Parameters of the FFT performed by the oscilloscope.}
  \label{fig:fourier-params}
\end{figure}
For following calculations, the bandwidth per bin $\Delta f = \SI{5}{\Hz}$ is important.

Again, an averaging over time is performed to filter out any temporary effects.
The resulting time-averaged FFT is shown in \figref{fig:noise-opa}, with a cursor marking the noise at \SI{1}{\kHz}.
The oscilloscope shows the corresponding voltage value as \texttt{V1}.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.6\textwidth]{noise-opa2}
  \caption{FFT of the op amp circuit's output, with the input connected to ground.}
  \label{fig:noise-opa}
\end{figure}
This value is $U_\text{tot}$, the combination of the noise $U_\text{OPA}$
on the output of the op amp circuit and the noise $U_\text{scope}$ due to the
oscilloscope measuring and digitizing the signal. To measure $U_\text{scope}$
we connect the osilloscope's input to ground. The spectrum of the grounded input can be seen
in \figref{fig:noise-scope}.
 
$U_\text{OPA}$ and $U_\text{scope}$ are two independent noise sources so
their values add quadratically:
\begin{equation}
  % U_\text{tot} = \sqrt{U_\text{OPA}^2 + U_\text{scope}^2}
  U_\text{tot}^2 = {U_\text{OPA}^2 + U_\text{scope}^2} \label{eq:total-noise}
\end{equation}
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.6\textwidth]{noise-scope4}
  \caption{Noise measurement of the oscilloscope. The grounded input is on channel 2 (blue)
    and its FFT on \textit{Math1} (purple).}
  \label{fig:noise-scope}
\end{figure}

We read $U_\text{tot} = \SI{25.15(50)}{\uV}$ from \figref{fig:noise-opa} and
$U_\text{scope} = \SI{3.35(20)}{\uV}$ from \figref{fig:noise-scope},
where the errors are estimated based on how much the measurements varied over time
while performing the experiment. Rearranging \eqref{eq:total-noise}, we get
\begin{equation*}
  U_\text{OPA} = \sqrt{U_\text{tot}^2 - U_\text{scope}^2} = \SI{24.93(51)}{\uV}
\end{equation*}
where the uncertainty was calculated using error propagation.

According to \cite{noise-analysis}, spot noise is the
\enquote{noise power normalized to \SI{1}{\Hz} bandwidth (at a particular center frequency)},
i.e. $\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta f}$, which is proportional to $\frac{\Delta U^2}{\Delta f}$.
In our case $\Delta U = U_\text{OPA}$ and $\Delta f = \SI{5}{\Hz}$.
When considering sources of noise in the context of a circuit, it becomes useful to describe
them by noise voltage instead of power. The voltage spot noise density $E_O$ (\textit{O} for \textit{output}) 
is then then proportional to
the square root of the power density and can be calculated using the values given above:
\begin{equation}
  E_O = \sqrt{\frac{\Delta U^2}{\Delta f}} = \frac{\Delta U}{\sqrt{\Delta f}} =
    \frac{U_\text{OPA}}{\sqrt{\Delta f}} = \SI{11.15(23)}{\uV\per\sqrt{\Hz}}
\end{equation}

$E_O$ depends not only on the op amp but also on the components used in the circuit.
To be able compare our measurement to datasheet values,
we will calculate the input noise voltage $E_\text{NI}$ which is an intrinsic property of the op amp used.
The relevant relation is \cite{noise-analysis}
\begin{equation}
  E_O = \sqrt{(E_\text{NI}^2 + (I_\text{BN}R_s)^2 + 4kTR_s)G^2 + (I_\text{BI}R_f)^2 + 4kTR_fG}
\end{equation}
Where $R_f$ and $G$ are the values from section \ref{setup}; $R_s = 0$ for our circuit; $I_\text{BI}$ and $I_\text{BN}$ are
the op amp's inverting and non-inverting input noise current, respectively;
$k$ is the Boltzmann constant; and $T$ is the temperature of the circuit.

Estimating $T=\SI{290}{\K}$ and using $4kT = \SI{1.6e-20}{\J} \cdot \frac{T}{\SI{290}{\K}} = \SI{1.6e-20}{\J}$
\cite{noise-analysis}, we have $4kTR_fG \approx \SI{1.6e-12}{\V\squared\per\Hz} = \SI{1.6e+6}{\nV\squared\per\Hz}$.
The OPA37 has a bipolar input stage meaning that the inverting and non-inverting input noise current are the
same \cite{noise-analysis}. At \SI{1}{\kHz} they have a value of \SI{0.4}{\pA\per\sqrt{\Hz}},
so $(I_\text{BI}R_f)^2 \approx (\SI{40}{\nV\per\sqrt{\Hz}})^2 = \SI{1.6e3}{\nV\squared\per\Hz}$
which can be neglected compared to the other term. Using this approximation and $R_s = 0$, we get
\begin{align*}
  E_O^2 = (E_\text{NI}G)^2 + 4kTR_fG
  \implies &E_\text{NI} = \frac{1}{G}\sqrt{E_O^2 - 4kTR_fG} = \SI{11.06(23)}{\nV\per\sqrt{\Hz}}.
\end{align*}
According to the OPA37 datasheet \cite{datasheet} the typical $E_O$ value at \SI{1}{\kHz} is \SI{3.2}{\nV\per\sqrt{\Hz}}
and the maximum is \SI{4.5}{\nV\per\sqrt{\Hz}}. Our measurement is larger than the maximum by a factor $\sim 2.5$.
This large discrepancy must be caused by some systematic error or a noise source that was not accounted for.
Some possibilities are external noise, e.\,g.\ due to external fields
leaking through the shielding, as well as discrepancies due to aging of the used components.



\section{Conclusion}
In this experiment, the gain of a non-inverting operational amplifier circuit as well as its output spot noise density
were measured. The measured gain is about \SI{6}{\percent} larger than the value expected from the resistor values,
which is likely due to imprecisions of the resistors.

The voltage spot noise density of the circuit's output at \SI{1}{\kHz} was measured using an oscilloscope with an FFT function
and found to be \SI{11.15(23)}{\uV\per\sqrt{\Hz}}. From this, the input noise voltage of the used op amp (Burr-Brown OPA37)
was calculated to be \SI{11.06(23)}{\nV\per\sqrt{\Hz}} which is significantly higher than the maximum value
\SI{4.5}{\nV\per\sqrt{\Hz}} given in the datasheet. There are various possible causes for this discrepancy.
