\section*{Introduction}
Noise can occur in operational amplifiers (op amps; OPA) due to various effects of their internal components.
This noise is generally dependent on the frequency of operation.
The goal of this experiment is to measure the voltage spot noise density of a Burr-Brown OPA37 op amp
which corresponds to the amount of noise present at a specific frequency.

\end{titlepage}



\section{Setup}
The op amp is set up in a non-inverting amplifier circuit, see \figref{fig:opa-circuit}.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.5\textwidth]{opa-circuit}
  \caption{non-inverting amplifier circuit. $R_f = \SI{101}{\kohm}, R_g = \SI{101}{\ohm}$ \cite{manual}}
  \label{fig:opa-circuit}
\end{figure}
The expected gain is $G=\frac{U_\text{in}}{U_\text{out}} = 1+\frac{R_f}{R_g}=1001$.
The capacitor in parallel to $R_f$ effectively lowers the resistance and therefore the gain for high frequencies,
acting as a low pass filter.
This prevents aliasing effects due to sampling of high frequencies \cite{manual}.

The input of the circuit is fed by a signal generator and the output is measured using an oscilloscope.



\section{Gain Measurement}
First, the gain of the circuit is measured by comparing the
input signal from the signal generator and the amplified output on the oscilloscope.
Two measurements are shown in \figref{fig:gain-measurement}.
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \begin{minipage}{.5\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{gain1.jpg}
  \end{minipage}%
  \begin{minipage}{.5\textwidth}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{gain2.jpg}
  \end{minipage}
  \caption{Gain measurements of the operational amplifier circuit.
    The input to the circuit is on channel 1 (yellow), the output on channel 2 (blue)
    and \textit{Math1} (orange) shows the average of channel 1 over time.}
  \label{fig:gain-measurement}
\end{figure}

Due to the high gain of the op amp circuit, the input has a low amplitude and thus a
low signal-to-noise ratio.
To avoid exaggerating the measured peak-to-peak amplitude of the input due to noise,
the average of multiple waveforms is taken. The measured amplitudes still vary over time.
To calculate the measured gain, the mean values of the amplitudes of the two
measurements in \figref{fig:gain-measurement} are used:
\begin{equation*}
  G = \frac{\overline{U}_\text{out,\,pp}}{\overline{U}_\text{in,\,pp}} = \frac{\SI{5.72}{\V} + \SI{5.64}{\V}}{\SI{5.404}{\mV} + \SI{5.368}{\mV}} \approx 1058
\end{equation*}
This value is about \SI{6}{\percent} larger than the value of $1001$ calculated from the resistor values.
This is likely due to imprecision of the actual resistance values.



\section{Voltage Spot Noise Measurement}
The main task of the experiment is to measure the voltage spot noise
at a frequency of \SI{1}{\kHz} and calculate the op amp's input noise voltage $E_\text{NI}$ from it.
To do this, the waveform generator is turned of and the op amp circuit's input connected to ground.
The output is again measured on the oscilloscope, which can be configured to calculate the fast fourier transform (FFT)
of the waveform. The result is shown in \figref{fig:noise-opa}, with a cursor marking the noise at \SI{1}{\kHz}.
\begin{figure}
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.6\textwidth]{noise-opa2}
  \caption{FFT of the op amp circuit's output, with the input connected to ground.}
  \label{fig:noise-opa}
\end{figure}
This value is $U_\text{tot}$, the combination of the noise $U_\text{OPA}$
on the output of the op amp circuit and the noise $U_\text{scope}$ due to the
oscilloscope measuring and digitizing the signal. These are two independent noise sources so
their values add quadratically:
\begin{equation}
  U_\text{tot} = \sqrt{U_\text{OPA}^2 + U_\text{scope}^2}
\end{equation}
